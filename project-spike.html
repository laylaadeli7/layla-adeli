Neural Spike Detector ASIC

Verilog ASIC Tiny Tapeout cocotb Signal Processing

Overview

Designed and taped out a digital neural spike detector in Verilog, fabricated on GlobalFoundries' 180nm process via the Tiny Tapeout GF26b shuttle, with chips expected back in November 2026. The chip implements a complete real-time pipeline for detecting neural action potentials in a sampled signal: a pipelined IIR bandpass filter, a nonlinear energy operator (NEO) for spike emphasis, and an adaptive EWMA-based threshold for detection — all with parameters configurable over SPI.

Pipelined IIR Bandpass Filter

The front end is an infinite impulse response (IIR) bandpass filter that isolates the frequency band where extracellular action potentials live, rejecting low-frequency drift and high-frequency noise outside the band of interest. The filter's datapath is pipelined so that each stage of the multiply-accumulate chain completes within a single clock cycle, allowing the design to run at the target clock rate without becoming the critical timing path.

Nonlinear Energy Operator (NEO)

Filtered samples are passed through a nonlinear energy operator, which combines a signal sample with its neighbors to produce an instantaneous estimate of signal energy. This nonlinear transform sharply emphasizes the fast, high-amplitude transients characteristic of action potentials relative to background noise, making spikes far easier to separate from baseline activity than they would be in the raw filtered signal.

Adaptive EWMA Threshold

Rather than detecting against a fixed threshold, the design tracks an exponentially weighted moving average (EWMA) of the NEO output to continuously estimate the local noise floor. A spike is flagged when the signal exceeds this adaptive baseline by a configurable margin, allowing the detector to stay robust as background noise levels drift over time.

SPI-Configurable Parameters

Filter coefficients, EWMA decay rate, and detection sensitivity are all exposed through a SPI interface, so the detector's behavior can be tuned post-fabrication without resynthesizing the design — useful for adapting to different electrode/noise conditions during testing.

Timing Closure

Post-synthesis static timing analysis revealed timing closure violations in the filter and NEO datapath. These were diagnosed and resolved by inserting additional pipeline registers along the critical path, trading a small amount of latency for a design that closed timing reliably at the target clock frequency.

Verification

Functional correctness was verified with cocotb and Icarus Verilog testbenches at the RTL level, then re-run against the post-layout gate-level netlist to confirm that synthesis and place-and-route preserved correct behavior, including the pipelining changes introduced during timing closure.

Design Challenges

The main challenge was closing timing on the filter and NEO datapath without disrupting the carefully tuned detection behavior. Particular care was needed for:

  • Adding pipeline stages without shifting the relative timing between the filtered signal, NEO output, and EWMA threshold comparison.
  • Re-verifying detection accuracy after each pipelining change, since added latency could otherwise desynchronize the threshold comparison from the signal it was meant to gate.
  • Confirming gate-level behavior matched RTL behavior post-layout, where parasitic delays can expose timing issues invisible at the RTL stage.
