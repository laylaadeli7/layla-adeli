8 GHz Delay Lock Loop

Cadence Virtuoso Spectre 45nm CMOS Maestro Analog IC Design

Overview

Designed a low-power, low-jitter 8 GHz Delay Lock Loop (DLL) in 45nm CMOS technology for EE124 (Mixed-mode Integrated Circuits). The DLL synchronizes a clock signal with minimal phase offset and jitter using a pass-transistor PFD and an 8-cell current-starved VCDL — critical in high-speed communication and processor clock distribution [1]. Simulation results confirmed stable phase lock for all required input phases (0, π/2, π, 3π/2) within a 5 ns rise time and settling window, under both ideal and noisy supply/input conditions.

Phase-Frequency Detector (PFD)

A pass-transistor-based PFD detects the phase and frequency difference between the reference clock and feedback signal, generating UP/DOWN correction pulses [2][3]. Implemented with a pair of D flip-flops and a reset path to resolve the dead-zone and minimize static phase offset. The architecture was selected for its low power draw and compatibility with the high-frequency operating target.

fig 1. Phase-frequency detector schematic

Charge Pump & Loop Filter (CP/LF)

The charge pump converts PFD UP/DOWN pulses into a smooth control voltage Vctrl. The loop filter capacitor value and charge pump current were sized to achieve a stable lock within the 5 ns window, with sufficient phase margin to avoid oscillation. The mirrored biasing scheme keeps current consumption low while maintaining symmetry between charge and discharge paths [1].

fig 2. Charge pump and loop filter schematic

Voltage-Controlled Delay Line (VCDL)

An 8-cell cascaded current-starved delay line whose per-cell delay is tuned by Vctrl from the loop filter. The total VCDL delay is driven to exactly one reference clock period, locking the output phases at 0°, 90°, 180°, and 270°. A non-starved inverter buffer (M10, M11) at the output restores a near-square-wave output and maintains switching symmetry. The Vctrl-to-delay characteristic was verified across the full operating range [4].

fig 3. Voltage-controlled delay line element schematic

Simulation Results — Case I: No Noise

Initial characterization was performed under ideal conditions (no supply or input noise) to establish baseline locking performance. Vctrl achieved a smooth, well-damped transition to steady state for all four phases within 5 ns, locking at 0.605 V, 0.560 V, 0.540 V, and 0.517 V for 0°, 90°, 180°, and 270° respectively — consistent with the expected delay-vs-Vctrl curve.

The averaged phase transfer characteristic showed output values of −0.005π, 0.495π, 1.028π, and 1.510π against target inputs of 0, π/2, π, and 3π/2 — a maximum average displacement of 0.030π (1.87 ps) at the π phase shift, validating the precision of transistor sizing and CP current symmetry. Eye diagrams confirmed significant eye openings with sub-picosecond peak-to-peak jitter across all phases.

fig 4. Averaged φin vs. φout — high linearity across the full 2π range

Simulation Results — Case II: Supply & Input Noise

Under noisy conditions (noise injected on Vin0 and Vsup), the DLL maintained stable lock for all four phases within the same 5 ns window. Vctrl exhibited visible high-frequency ripple at steady state — a direct consequence of supply noise coupling through the mirrored biasing — but the loop remained well-damped and required no re-sizing.

Noise increased timing uncertainty significantly: peak-to-peak jitter rose to a maximum of 12.47 ps at the 90° phase shift, and RMS jitter peaked at 3.26 ps. Despite this, duty cycle distortion remained below 1% across all cases (within ±1% of the 50% ideal), and power consumption stayed under 3.8 mW — well within the 8 mW specification. The eye diagrams maintained strong horizontal and vertical openings across all four output phases.

Outcome

Awarded Best DLL Project in EE124 for best combined performance across power consumption, phase offset, and peak-to-peak jitter among class submissions. All target specifications were met in both ideal and noisy conditions.
